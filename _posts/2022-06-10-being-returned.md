I recently watched a video made by an amazing philosophical youtuber, Einzelgänger, in which he explores the idea of loss from a stoic perspective. The video can be found at:
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yah-abKb96g&ab_channel=Einzelg%C3%A4nger

The video itself is straightforward, really only talking about our attachments to other people and things, and how it is better to think of our losses rather as returns. While watching the video, a question began to form. One that couldn't simply be answered with a simple google search.

#### What about the returned?

We return others to nature, but what truly happens to those who are returned? I'm not just talking about the ones who die, but about the ones who simply leave, or the ones that we lose contact with. *Do they think about us in the way we think of them? Do they think they have returned us as well?* It also brings into question whether it is virtuous or not to regain contact with them. Perhaps the desire to regain contact is simply a desire to feel that you are correct, and are helping another. I found myself continuing to think about these ideas, but with a stoic perspective:

#### Should we care?

Truly, think of it. Let's say you've bought a shirt from a store, but the shirt turns out to be underwhelming, or you simply don't want it anymore. You head on down to the store you bought it at, show them the receipt and they take it away, never to be seen by you again.  We no longer care about the item, since we no longer see it. It frees up a little bit in our mind, and we can worry on something else. (like being virtuous)

Once someone or something has left our life, and has been "returned", we should no longer worry about it, as it's no longer in our control. While we are in possession of such things, they should be considered to be things for us to enact our virtue upon, but once we have "returned" it, we cannot control them, nor enact our virtue upon them, thus any actions relating to them should be considered a part of adiaphora.

This entire monologue reminds me of a stoic quote by Epictetus, "Don’t wish for things to be a certain way. Wish them to be as they are." We should not wish to right those who have been returned, rather we should wish them well and move on.

All in all, the lesson learned here is that the answer to a question can be another question.
